Persepolis: a Linked Open Data project (20-entity edition)

Persepolis was the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Empire, founded by Cyrus the Great and built as a monumental complex by Darius the Great beginning in 518 BCE, in what is today Fars Province, near the modern city of Marvdasht. Before Darius chose this site, the Achaemenid kings ruled from Pasargadae, the empire's first capital, which Cyrus had built roughly forty kilometres to the northeast. Darius commissioned the site's grandest buildings, including the Tachara, his private palace, and began the Apadana, the great audience hall, which his son and successor Xerxes I completed and further expanded with the construction of the Gate of All Nations. The kings whose likenesses appear throughout the site practiced Zoroastrianism, and the trilingual royal inscriptions carved into its walls, written partly in Old Persian, represent some of the earliest deciphered examples of that language. Nearby, at Naqsh-e Rustam, the tombs of Darius and Xerxes were cut into a cliff face overlooking the plain. In 330 BCE, Alexander the Great captured and burned Persepolis during his conquest of the Achaemenid Empire, effectively ending organized Persian resistance in the region. The ruins lay largely unexcavated until the twentieth century, when the German archaeologist Ernst Herzfeld began systematic excavations in 1931 on behalf of the Institute for the Study of Ancient Cultures at the University of Chicago; Herzfeld's team uncovered the Persepolis Administrative Archives, a collection of clay tablets documenting the empire's daily administration. Herzfeld was succeeded as expedition director by Erich Schmidt in 1934, who continued the excavations until 1939. In recognition of its outstanding cultural value, UNESCO inscribed Persepolis on the World Heritage List in 1979 [src].
