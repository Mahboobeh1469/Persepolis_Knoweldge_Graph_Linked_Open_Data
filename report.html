1. The idea

The starting point of this project is the English Wikipedia page on Persepolis, the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Empire in present-day Iran. Persepolis was chosen because, unlike many single archaeological sites, it sits at the center of a genuinely rich network: it has a founder and a builder-successor, a predecessor capital, a destroyer, a modern excavation history with named archaeologists and a sponsoring institution, a religious and linguistic context, and a UNESCO recognition — enough well-documented, independently notable entities to build a non-trivial, defensible Linked Open Data graph without stretching for weak connections.

2. Entity selection and reconciliation

Twenty entities were selected and individually verified against live Wikidata and English Wikipedia pages — no QID was taken from memory or guessed. Five categories beyond the obvious "site + rulers" were deliberately included to make the graph more than a star topology: the predecessor capital and its founder (Cyrus, Pasargadae), the royal necropolis (Naqsh-e Rustam), the excavation history (Herzfeld, Schmidt, the sponsoring institute), and the religious/linguistic/ documentary context (Zoroastrianism, Old Persian, the Fortification Archive).

3. Theoretical model

The diagram below shows, in natural-language form, how the twenty entities relate to Persepolis and to each other. Historically sensitive predicates were chosen deliberately: Alexander the Great is destroyedBy, never "built by"; Xerxes I is expandedBy (he continued and completed construction, he did not found the site); the Achaemenid Empire is what Persepolis is capitalOf, not something that "built" Persepolis. Secondary, non-hub relationships are also present: Marvdasht is locatedIn Fars Province directly; Pasargadae was builtBy Cyrus and was itself capitalOf the Achaemenid Empire before Persepolis succeeds it; Naqsh-e Rustam containsTomb of both Darius and Xerxes; Erich Schmidt succeededExcavationOf Ernst Herzfeld; and the Fortification Archive was discoveredBy Herzfeld. Entities are color-coded by type, and four historical events (Foundation 518 BCE, Destruction 330 BCE, Excavation 1931–1939, Inscription 1979) are shown as separate diamond nodes with their own dates.

Every edge carries a visible arrowhead and follows one of three consistent line styles, explained in the diagram's own legend: solid for the 18 relations that directly involve Persepolis, dashed for the 12 secondary relations between two other entities (e.g. Marvdasht↔Fars, Apadana↔Darius), and dotted for the connectors linking entities to the four historical events. The same three-tier convention is reused identically in conceptual_model.svg below, so the two diagrams never disagree about which relationships are direct versus secondary.

4. Conceptual model / ontology

The theoretical model was formalized by reusing existing vocabularies rather than inventing a new schema:

CIDOC-CRM for cultural-heritage classes: crm:E27_Site, crm:E22_Human-Made_Object, crm:E18_Physical_Thing (their shared superclass), crm:E4_Period, crm:E53_Place, crm:E56_Language, crm:E31_Document, and crm:E5_Event with subclasses crm:E63_Beginning_of_Existence, crm:E6_Destruction, crm:E7_Activity — the international standard ontology for archaeological/cultural heritage documentation.

FOAF for people and organizations (foaf:Person, foaf:Organization).

Dublin Core Terms for generic descriptive/relational properties reused directly: dcterms:description, dcterms:spatial, dcterms:hasPart, dcterms:type, dcterms:date, dcterms:language (as a superproperty), plus dcterms:creator, dcterms:contributor and dcterms:replaces as superproperties of several ex: terms.

SKOS for Zoroastrianism as a controlled concept (skos:Concept).

OWL/RDFS for 19 project-specific object properties, each declared rdfs:subPropertyOf an existing Dublin Core Terms property: ex:builtBy, ex:expandedBy, ex:completedBy, ex:destroyedBy, ex:capitalOf, ex:succeeds, ex:locatedNear, ex:recognizes, ex:foundedBy, ex:containsTomb, ex:excavatedBy, ex:succeededExcavationOf, ex:conductedExcavationAt, ex:associatedWithReligion, ex:hasLanguage, ex:yieldedArchive, ex:discoveredBy, plus dcterms:spatial and dcterms:hasPart reused directly without a custom wrapper.

Why reuse instead of inventing a new ontology: reusing CIDOC-CRM, FOAF, Dublin Core and SKOS means the dataset is immediately interoperable with any other Linked Data consumer that already understands these (very widely deployed) vocabularies, avoids re-solving modeling problems that domain experts already solved, and keeps the amount of genuinely new vocabulary small and easy to justify — only 19 narrow, clearly-scoped properties were minted, each as a sub-property of an existing term rather than a free-standing invention. Cardinality per property (e.g. hasPart is 1..*, a site can have many components, while foundedBy is 1..1) is documented as rdfs:comment annotations directly in generate_rdf.py's ontology block.

The diagram below follows the CIDOC-CRM/FRBRoo instance-graph convention used in professional Semantic Web visualizations: each yellow rectangle is a class (a reused vocabulary term such as crm:E27_Site or foaf:Person), each small pink circle is an individual — one of the 20 real project entities or 4 events, attached to the class it is an instance of — and each blue-labeled arrow is an object property connecting two specific instances (e.g. persepolis — ex:builtBy → darius). This makes the class/individual distinction explicit: the same class (foaf:Person) recurs as a separate box for each of the six people, exactly mirroring how knowledge_graph.ttl actually types each resource. Every arrow carries a visible arrowhead and follows the identical three-tier line style used in theoretical_model.svg above: solid blue for the 18 direct relationships involving Persepolis, dashed blue for the 12 secondary relationships between two other entities, and dotted gray for the event-linking properties reused directly from CIDOC-CRM (crm:P11_had_participant, crm:P92i_was_brought_into_existence_by, crm:P93i_was_taken_out_of_existence_by, crm:P7_took_place_at).

5. TEI/XML encoding

The file data.xml encodes a short, originally worded descriptive paragraph about Persepolis (informed by, but not copied from, the Wikipedia article) and marks up all twenty entities. Each entity is described once in a <standOff> section — listPlace, listPerson, listOrg, a monuments list, a concepts list, listBibl (for the document archive), and listEvent — and referenced from the running text via the @ref attribute on <placeName>, <persName>, <orgName> and <name> elements. All 30 relationships from the theoretical model are additionally encoded as structured <listRelation> statements, which is what generate_rdf.py reads directly to build the RDF dataset — so the same relations shown in the diagrams also exist as machine-readable data.

6. XML → HTML transformation

The stylesheet style.xsl transforms data.xml into a readable HTML page: the narrative text with entities shown as highlighted links, followed by tables for each entity type (Persons, Places, Organizations, Monuments, Concepts, Document Archive) and a full 30-row relationship table. It was applied with transform.py, a short Python script using the lxml library (which wraps the standard libxslt processor), producing output.html.

Simple explanation: XSLT is a rule-based language that says "when you see this XML element, produce this HTML element." data.xml is transformed into HTML because XML is good for structured data but not pleasant to read directly — the HTML output is what a human, like your professor, actually looks at. output.html is that human-readable rendering.

7. TEI → RDF transformation

generate_rdf.py parses data.xml with lxml and serializes knowledge_graph.ttl in Turtle syntax: the ontology declarations (section 4), one resource per entity (20, each with owl:sameAs to Wikidata and foaf:isPrimaryTopicOf to Wikipedia), four event resources with dates and crm:P11_had_participant links, and 30 relation triples matching listRelation exactly — there is no contradiction between the XML, the RDF, the diagrams, and this report, since three of the four are generated directly from data.xml by code, not written by hand separately.

res:persepolis ex:builtBy res:darius .
res:persepolis ex:expandedBy res:xerxes .
res:alexander a foaf:Person ; owl:sameAs <https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q8409> .
res:persepolis ex:destroyedBy res:alexander .
res:unesco ex:recognizes res:persepolis .

Simple explanation: RDF (Resource Description Framework) represents facts as triples — subject, predicate, object, e.g. "Persepolis / builtBy / Darius." Turtle is a compact text syntax for writing RDF triples. owl:sameAs states that two URIs refer to the same real-world thing, which is how this dataset connects to Wikidata's much larger graph — that connection is what turns a private XML file into part of the open, global Knowledge Graph.

8. Knowledge graph

knowledge_graph.ttl is the project's Knowledge Graph: 20 entities + 4 events as nodes, 30 relation triples plus ontology/type/description triples as edges, all expressed with reused, standard vocabulary terms and grounded in Wikidata via owl:sameAs. It can be loaded into any RDF triple store (GraphDB, Apache Jena Fuseki) or pasted into a visual tool such as the RDF Grapher to inspect the graph structure directly.

11. Conclusion

This project follows LODLAM principles end to end: it starts from a single Wikipedia page, identifies a well-verified set of related entities (exceeding the minimum of 10, with 20), reconciles every one of them to Wikidata, models their relationships first informally and then formally with reused vocabularies (CIDOC-CRM, FOAF, Dublin Core, SKOS), encodes the source text in TEI/XML with proper named-entity annotation, and mechanically derives both a human-readable HTML page and a machine-readable RDF/Turtle knowledge graph from that single XML source — so nothing in the diagrams, the report, or the RDF was written independently or could silently drift out of sync with the underlying data.
